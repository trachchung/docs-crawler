# The AI Creative Destruction Wave
Why entire companies will vanish, not just jobs
July 8, 2025
[ #ai](https://danielmiessler.com/archives/?tag=ai)[ #future](https://danielmiessler.com/archives/?tag=future)[ #business](https://danielmiessler.com/archives/?tag=business)[ #society](https://danielmiessler.com/archives/?tag=society)[ #top](https://danielmiessler.com/archives/?tag=top)
 Amor-fati-ing…
The scariest thing to me about AI is that I think very few of the current companies and products that we use everyday are the best solutions.
Current companies are inefficient versions of what they could be. And current products are bad versions of what they could be.
This was never a problem before because _everything_ is like that. Every company making every product. We're all struggling with efficiency and bloat and overhead and bureaucracy.
The real disruption isn't AI replacing workers - it's AI replacing the companies and products and services that employ them.
This is what scares me.
I think we're about to see millions of jobs lost—not just because the companies and products that survive will replace many workers with AI.
What I'm really worried about is those companies and services and products disappearing altogether.
## The New Competitive Reality [​](https://danielmiessler.com/blog/ai-creative-destruction-wave#the-new-competitive-reality)
I'm worried about how many of them will be replaced by brand new products and services that do the job much better, and iterate far faster, because the "team" that made them is actually just 1 or a handful of people who are shipping features almost instantly.
Think about what this means. A traditional SaaS company might have:
  * Product managers
  * Designers
  * Frontend developers
  * Backend developers
  * QA teams
  * Support teams
  * Marketing teams
  * Sales teams


Now imagine a competitor with 2-3 people using AI to handle most of these functions. They can:
  * Ship features in hours instead of months
  * Pivot instantly based on customer feedback
  * Operate at a fraction of the cost
  * Price their product 90% cheaper

When your operational costs drop by 95%, you can undercut competitors while maintaining better margins. This isn't theoretical - it's happening now.
## The Protected and the Vulnerable [​](https://danielmiessler.com/blog/ai-creative-destruction-wave#the-protected-and-the-vulnerable)
Of course there are tons of businesses and products that have moats of different kinds, and that this won't happen to. Companies making hardware. Companies where it's almost impossible to switch to a competitor.
But I think most products and companies—especially SaaS companies—are about to face existential pressure from new AI offerings that are just better and cheaper.
It won't just be people losing jobs because their company can automate their role. Their companies might not exist anymore.
Consider the typical B2B SaaS company:
  * High customer acquisition costs
  * Complex onboarding processes
  * Armies of customer success managers
  * Endless feature requests backlog
  * Slow development cycles


Now imagine a lean AI-powered alternative that:
  * Onboards customers automatically
  * Provides instant 24/7 support
  * Ships requested features in days
  * Costs 1/10th the price


## Security Companies: A Prime Example [​](https://danielmiessler.com/blog/ai-creative-destruction-wave#security-companies-a-prime-example)
This creative destruction is especially devastating for companies I know best—those in cybersecurity. So many security products are built on the "bolt-on" concept, doing security separately from the initial engineering workflow.
My friend Caleb Sima put it perfectly: "Engineering should eat security."
The ideal isn't to take security things and put them into engineering. It's far more revolutionary—the security things become engineering things.
During my time at ExxonMobil, I learned it's all about pursuing an ideal process. Think about civil engineering—they don't bolt on safety to a skyscraper design. The way to build safely IS the way to build. It's baked into the SOP (Standard Operating Procedure). [I've written about how AI-powered SOPs will transform security and business](https://danielmiessler.com/blog/policy-sops-and-ai-are-all-you-need).
What AI enables is revolutionary. Imagine submitting code where:
  * 100 different AI agents automatically build the app end-to-end
  * They test with and without your PR implemented
  * Dynamic testing, static analysis, functionality testing—all happen automatically
  * Everything is integrated directly with GitHub or your build system


This becomes the new way of building. Not a separate security step, but THE engineering process itself.
New AI-powered products won't add security layers—they'll replace how we build software entirely. And they'll eat every company still doing it the old way.
The troubling part? Most existing security companies are based on doing this incorrectly—operating outside the engineering workflow.
And they are in serious trouble.
## The Timeline Is Terrifying [​](https://danielmiessler.com/blog/ai-creative-destruction-wave#the-timeline-is-terrifying)
I think AI is going to remake things. It's like a giant reset. For a lot of things like government I'm eager to see the changes.
But I'm very concerned that the replacement of these companies and products and services is going to abruptly shock the labor market in a way that we've never seen before.
Unfortunately, there doesn't need to be some giant movement for this to happen. It's not like a bunch of people are gonna coordinate and say hey, let's do this.
It's a whole bunch of tiny cuts happening independently.
## Death by a Thousand Startups [​](https://danielmiessler.com/blog/ai-creative-destruction-wave#death-by-a-thousand-startups)
Every week, another tiny team launches something that makes an entire category of software obsolete.
  * That project management tool your company pays $50/user/month for? Someone just built a better one for $5.
  * The customer support platform requiring a team of 10 to manage? Now it's an AI agent that needs zero management.
  * The data analytics dashboard that took 6 months to implement? Built in a weekend by one person.

The scariest part is that these aren't inferior products. They're often better because they're built from scratch with modern assumptions.
Each one seems insignificant. But collectively, they're hollowing out entire industries.
## Why Listen to Me? [​](https://danielmiessler.com/blog/ai-creative-destruction-wave#why-listen-to-me)
But wait, why do I have an opinion on this? And why should you consider anything I say?
First off, I could be wrong, and no amount of experience can negate that. But for those skeptical of my perspective and/or experience, I've consulted for hundreds of companies across multiple industries in the last 25 years. I've worked full-time at companies like Apple and HP, and have seen a lot under the covers of what makes companies move slowly. And that's why I'm worried.
I think AI changes the fundamental economics of software creation and competition.
When one person can build what used to take a team of 50, and when AI can handle what used to require entire departments, the old way of doing business is going to face extraordinary pressure.
This isn't about whether AI will take your job. It's about whether your entire company, or product, has a reason to exist at all in a market full of options that move faster than you.
And this doesn't need to happen all at once. It's not like there's going to be some meeting where people decide to replace "old" products and services. And companies aren't going to decide to lay off workers en masse.
But all of them individually are now facing that pressure. And I think a large number of them will either go out of business or lay off many or most of their people.
I'm worried. Let's hope I'm wrong.
